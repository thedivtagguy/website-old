Course abstracts in design schools have a way of saying things without telling you what they mean. I am all for the use of good language and metaphors, but I sometimes don't quite know what to make of paragraphs that {{< sidenote "left" "left-note" "go like this" >}} Any resemblance to anything at all is *coughs* purely coincidental. {{< /sidenote >}}:

The cloud is a structure woven into air that we breathe. It makes intersections where there are none and brings into connection disparate objects; a branch, a roof, leaf, a pole, scooter and soil. And all this ceases to exist as the wind blows. This studio looks at impermanence and the various forms it takes. What does it mean? What will I do for the next one month? Who knows?

Ambiguity is the name of the game, and I believe the power to be ambiguious should reside with the people. I demand to be ambiguious about things that I care about too. Ambiguity is not a resource of the bourgeoisie. The proletariat should seize the means of ambiguity. I proclaim Liberté, égalité, ambiguïté.

If clouds were good enough, then if I felt like attending a class about cauliflowers, I should be able to. Same goes for bubbles. Or moustaches. Or bellybuttons.

So I trained an AI model using GPT-3, one of the most sophisticated text-generation models available right now, to generate a course abstract for anything I wanted. Here are the results.
